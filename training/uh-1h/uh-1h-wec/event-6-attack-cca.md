## Learning Objectives

Upon completion of this event the student will be able to:

- [ ] Describe all four primary attack profiles and select the appropriate profile for a given tactical scenario
- [ ] Execute a wagon wheel to coordinate with a two-ship element; manage timing and spacing
- [ ] State the five elements of a fire request in the correct order
- [ ] Respond to a CCA talk-on; identify a target described by a controller; call *"Tally"* or *"No joy"*
- [ ] Execute a cleared-hot call correctly; respond to an abort call on the attack run
- [ ] Conduct an LZ prep engagement sequence against a designated area target; coordinate with ground elements

## Part 1 — Attack Profile Review

### Pre-Departure Quiz

IP briefs four scenarios; student selects the appropriate profile and states the key parameters (altitude, airspeed, depression):

| Scenario | Correct Profile | Key Parameters |
|---|---|---|
| Enemy troops in open; friendly forces 400 m away; flat terrain | Level / running fire | 200–300 ft AGL; 80 KIAS; 35 mils |
| Single enemy vehicle; accurate position; need precision | 20° dive | 1,500 ft AGL orbit; 80 KIAS; 22 mils at 1,000 m |
| Target offset 20° from nose; low altitude; no orbit room | Pedal turn | 200 ft AGL; 60 KIAS; 35 mils; yaw to align |
| Area suppression; multiple targets; medium accuracy acceptable | 10° dive | 1,000–1,200 ft AGL orbit; 80 KIAS; 26 mils at 800 m |

All four must be correct before proceeding.

## Part 2 — Wagon Wheel Execution

### Wagon Wheel Concept

The wagon wheel is a continuous attack pattern used by a single aircraft or a coordinated multi-ship element. It eliminates re-positioning time between passes by keeping the aircraft in a constant orbit around the target.

**Two-ship wagon wheel:**
- Both aircraft orbit the same point (the target).
- They are 90° apart in the orbit.
- When Lead rolls in to attack, Wing is 90° behind (still in the orbit).
- When Lead completes pull-off and climbs back to orbit altitude, Wing rolls in.
- This produces one attack approximately every 30–45 seconds, alternating between aircraft.

### Orbit Setup (Lead)

1. Lead identifies the target; announces: *"Warlord 6-1, establishing wagon wheel, target at [grid/bearing]. Orbit 1,200 ft AGL, 70 KIAS, right-hand."*
2. Wing acknowledges: *"Warlord 6-2, roger — joining trail. I'm at your 6 o'clock."*
3. Lead begins orbit; Wing joins 90° behind in the same orbit.
4. Lead confirms 90° spacing visually: *"6-2, I have you at my 9 o'clock — spacing good."*

### Roll-In Coordination

1. **Lead:** at the roll-in point: *"6-1 is hot."* (signals that Lead is committed to the attack run)
2. **Wing:** acknowledges: *"6-2 copies — holding orbit."*
3. **Lead:** completes attack; pull-off; climbs: *"6-1 is cold, climbing."*
4. **Wing:** at its roll-in point (90° later): *"6-2 is hot."*
5. Continue alternating.

> **"Hot" and "Cold":** These are the two required status calls during wagon wheel operations. "Hot" = on the attack run, trigger authorized. "Cold" = off the attack, safe, climbing. A ship is never "hot" unless the full arming checklist was complete before roll-in.

### Student Execution

1. Student (as Lead) sets up the orbit at 1,200 ft AGL; calls Wing.
2. Student executes 3 diving passes (10°, 80 KIAS); uses *"hot/cold"* calls on each.
3. IP (as Wing) executes 3 passes in coordination.
4. IP debrief: was spacing maintained? Were hot/cold calls timely? Did either ship re-attack while the other was still hot?

### Single-Ship Wagon Wheel

When operating single-ship, the wagon wheel is still flown identically — the aircraft orbits, rolls in, attacks, pulls off, and returns to orbit for the next pass. The terminology is the same; the pilot calls *"hot"* before rolling in (even solo) as a self-brief discipline check.

## Part 3 — CCA Fire Request Formats

### The Five Elements

A fire request sent to a CCA aircraft follows a standard five-element format. All five must be transmitted in order:

| Element | Content | Example |
|---|---|---|
| 1. IP/Offset | Initial Point and attack heading, or offset from IP | *"IP is the water tower; heading 270."* |
| 2. Hazards | Any threats, terrain, or restrictions | *"Threat: SA-7 south of IP. Terrain: ridgeline at 3 o'clock."* |
| 3. Target description | What the target is; what it looks like | *"Target: truck convoy; 4 vehicles in column; highway 3 km north of the water tower."* |
| 4. Target location | Grid or lat/long; elevation | *"Grid AB 123 456; elevation 80 meters."* |
| 5. Remarks | Special instructions; danger close; ROE; restrictions | *"Friendlies 500 m south. Cleared: rockets and minigun. No WP. Danger close not in effect."* |

### Student Fire Request Drill

IP presents a scenario; student transmits the complete fire request in the correct five-element order.

**Scenario (IP reads aloud):**

> Target: enemy logistics depot; three trucks parked near a barn; grid QR 448 221; elevation 120 m. Friendly forces 600 m to the southwest in a compound. Threat: reported small arms. Attack from the north; heading 180. Cleared to use all munitions. No danger close.

**Expected student transmission:**

> *"Warlord 6, Jester 3 — fire request. IP is the hilltop at [local landmark]; attack heading 180. Hazards: small arms south of target. Target: three trucks near a barn; grid QR 448 221, elevation 120 meters. Remarks: friendlies 600 meters southwest in compound — no danger close. Cleared: rockets, minigun, all munitions. How copy? Over."*

IP grades the request against the five-element standard. A complete, in-order transmission is a PASS. Any missing element or out-of-order transmission is an II.

## Part 4 — Talk-On & Cleared Hot Drill

### CCA Talk-On

The ground controller or JTAC may need to verbally guide the aircraft to a target that is difficult to locate from the air. This is a *talk-on*.

**Standard talk-on sequence:**

1. **Controller:** *"From the IP, fly heading 270 for 5 km. Target is a tree line."*
2. **Pilot:** flies inbound; searches.
3. **Controller:** *"Target is the east end of the tree line. You should have a grey barn at your 10 o'clock."*
4. **Pilot:** *"Tally barn."*
5. **Controller:** *"Target is 200 m north of that barn, on the tree line."*
6. **Pilot:** *"Tally target — visual on the tree line, small structure."*
7. **Controller:** *"That's the target. Report in-bound."*
8. **Pilot:** *"In-bound."*
9. **Controller:** *"Cleared hot."*
10. **Pilot:** *"Cleared hot — rolling in."*

### Key Terms

| Term | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Tally** | Visual contact with the target |
| **No joy** | Cannot see the target |
| **In-bound** | Aircraft is committed on the attack axis, approaching target |
| **Cleared hot** | Authorization to fire — the ground controller's clearance |
| **Abort** | Do not fire; cease attack immediately |
| **Terminate** | Cease all engagement; not just this pass — disengage |

### Student Drill (Two Talk-On Scenarios)

IP reads two talk-on scenarios; student responds with the correct terminology:

**Scenario A:** IP describes a target; student acquires it and calls *"Tally"* or *"No joy."*
**Scenario B:** IP clears student hot; student calls *"Cleared hot — rolling in."* IP then calls *"Abort"* at 500 ft during the dive. Student executes abort (see Part 5).

## Part 5 — Abort Procedures

### When to Abort

The pilot must abort an attack pass if:
- The controller calls *"Abort, abort"*
- The target is not identified before the fire point
- Friendly forces are observed in the target area
- A conflict with another aircraft is detected
- A system malfunction prevents safe employment
- The pilot loses situational awareness

### Abort Execution

When the abort call is received (or the pilot self-aborts):

1. **Release the trigger immediately** — if firing, stop.
2. **Pull off** — aft cyclic; climb; break away from the target.
3. **Call:** *"[Callsign] — aborting, off dry."* (*"Off dry"* = no rounds fired)
4. **Safe sequence** — Master Arm SAFE.
5. **Climb to orbit altitude** — re-establish a safe position.
6. **Report:** *"[Callsign] is cold. Reason for abort: [state reason]."*

### Self-Abort Criteria (Pilot Authority)

The pilot may self-abort at any time without waiting for a controller call. Self-abort criteria:

- Cannot positively identify the target (PID failure)
- Target not within the cleared parameters (location/description mismatch)
- Minimum altitude floor reached without firing (*"Floor — pulling off dry"*)
- Aircraft system malfunction

> **Zero Tolerance:** firing after an abort call is issued is a DOWN regardless of circumstances. There is no scenario in which continuing an attack after *"Abort, abort"* is acceptable.

### Abort Drill

IP will call abort at a random point during the student's attack run (before the fire point and again after fire point). Student must:
- Release trigger / not fire if before fire point
- Cease fire immediately if already firing
- Pull off; call *"Aborting, off dry / off wet"*

Both drills are graded.

## Part 6 — Combined Arms Pass (LZ Prep)

### LZ Preparation Concept

Before a helicopter assault landing, the LZ perimeter is suppressed by armed aircraft to clear potential threats. This is LZ prep.

**Standard LZ prep sequence (2-ship; 4 passes):**

1. Lead and Wing coordinate: each assigned two sides of the LZ perimeter.
2. **Pass 1 (Lead):** south edge of LZ; rockets; 10° dive.
3. **Pass 2 (Wing):** north edge; rockets; 10° dive. (simultaneous with Lead's pull-off/re-orbit)
4. **Pass 3 (Lead):** east edge; minigun or rockets.
5. **Pass 4 (Wing):** west edge; minigun or rockets.
6. Both aircraft: *"LZ prep complete — [assault ship callsign], LZ is clear. Proceed on your call."*

### Student Execution (Single-Ship Simulation)

If single-ship, the student covers all four sides in sequence; IP simulates the second ship and coordinates timing:

1. Student identifies LZ perimeter (IP marks it with smoke or brief description).
2. Student conducts 4 passes; one per side; 10° dive; rockets.
3. Between each pass: student calls *"Pass [N] complete; [N remaining]."*
4. After the 4th pass: student calls *"LZ prep complete — LZ is clear, recommend go for assault."*

### BDA (Battle Damage Assessment)

After LZ prep, the student orbits the LZ once at safe altitude and makes a BDA call:

> *"[Assault ship callsign], Warlord 6 — BDA: LZ perimeter suppressed, no observed return fire. No vehicles or personnel visible on LZ. LZ appears clear. Your call to proceed."*

## Completion Standards

| Standard | Criterion |
|---|---|
| Attack profile quiz | All 4 scenarios correct before departure |
| Wagon wheel — hot/cold calls | Made on every pass; no pass begun without *"hot"* call |
| Wagon wheel — spacing | 90° separation maintained; no two ships hot simultaneously |
| Fire request | All 5 elements transmitted in correct order |
| Talk-on — tally/no joy | Correct terminology; correct identification |
| Cleared hot call | *"Cleared hot"* used verbatim |
| Abort response — no fire | Trigger not pressed; *"aborting, off dry"* called |
| Abort response — cease fire | Trigger released within 1 second; *"aborting, off wet"* called |
| LZ prep | 4 passes completed; all four sides covered; LZ clear call made |
| BDA | BDA call made with content: observed threats; LZ status |

## Common Errors

| Error | Correction |
|---|---|
| No *"hot"* call before rolling in | Wagon wheel requires a hot call; enforce on every pass without exception |
| Two ships hot simultaneously | Brief 90° spacing requirement; both ships cannot be inbound at the same time |
| Fire request out of order | IP/Hazards/Target/Location/Remarks — drill until automatic |
| *"Clear"* instead of *"Cleared hot"* | Exact phrase matters; only *"Cleared hot"* authorizes fire |
| Continuing attack after *"Abort"* call | Automatic DOWN; re-brief the abort authority and zero-tolerance standard |
| LZ prep — all 4 passes from the same direction | Brief: one pass per side; each side gets its own angle of attack |
| BDA call too vague | Practice the format: threat status + vehicle/personnel status + LZ clear/unclear |
