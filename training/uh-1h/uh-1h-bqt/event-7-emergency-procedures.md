## Learning Objectives

Upon completion of this event the student will be able to:
- [ ] Recite all boldface/immediate-action emergency procedures from memory without reference to a checklist
- [ ] Execute a complete power-off autorotation from entry through power recovery (altitude) and from entry through touchdown (straight-in profile)
- [ ] Execute 90° and 180° autorotation profiles to a designated landing area
- [ ] Perform the engine fire in flight procedure and autorotate to landing
- [ ] Demonstrate LTE recognition and recovery: apply forward cyclic, reduce collective, maintain heading control
- [ ] Intentionally enter and recover from settling with power (VRS) at altitude; verbalize prevention techniques
- [ ] Manage governor failure: switch OFF, fly manually on throttle maintaining Nr 324 RPM, land as soon as practicable
- [ ] Manage hydraulic failure: correct airspeed range, plan and execute a run-on landing
- [ ] Describe electrical failure load-shedding priorities and battery endurance limitations
- [ ] Describe transmission malfunction indications and define "land as soon as possible" vs. "land as soon as practicable"
- [ ] Articulate mast bumping prevention mantra and recite the low-G recovery procedure
- [ ] Identify which emergencies DCS models and which are academic-only knowledge

## Ground Brief

### Boldface — Recite Before Flight

> **The IP will not brief the mission until the student successfully recites the following boldface from memory. No exceptions. No open-book assistance.**

#### ENGINE FAILURE IN FLIGHT (Autorotation Entry)
```
1. COLLECTIVE .............. LOWER (immediately — maintain Nr)
2. THROTTLE ................ CLOSE (full counterclockwise to OFF)
3. AIRSPEED ................ ESTABLISH 65 KIAS (autorotation glide)
4. PEDALS .................. MAINTAIN HEADING (right pedal — no torque)
5. Nr ...................... MAINTAIN 294-324 RPM (with collective)
6. SELECT LANDING AREA ..... Plan approach
7. EXECUTE AUTOROTATION .... Flare → Collective pull → Touchdown
```

#### ENGINE FAILURE AT HOVER / LOW ALTITUDE
```
1. COLLECTIVE .............. ADJUST to cushion landing
2. CYCLIC .................. MAINTAIN LEVEL ATTITUDE
3. PEDALS .................. MAINTAIN HEADING
4. THROTTLE ................ CLOSE (after touchdown)
```

#### ENGINE FIRE IN FLIGHT
```
1. THROTTLE ................ CLOSE (enter autorotation)
2. EMERGENCY FUEL SHUTOFF .. OFF
3. CABIN HEAT .............. OFF
4. LAND IMMEDIATELY ........ Autorotate to nearest suitable area
5. After landing: BATTERY .. OFF; egress immediately
```

#### FUEL CONTROL / GOVERNOR FAILURE
```
1. GOVERNOR (GOV) SWITCH ... OFF
2. THROTTLE ................ MANUAL — adjust to maintain Nr 324 RPM
3. COLLECTIVE .............. COORDINATE with throttle
4. LAND AS SOON AS PRACTICABLE
```

#### HYDRAULIC SYSTEM FAILURE
```
1. AIRSPEED ................ ADJUST to 60-80 KIAS
2. AVOID HOVER ............. Run-on landing recommended
3. HYD CONTROL SWITCH ...... OFF (if controls surge or uncommanded)
4. LAND AS SOON AS PRACTICABLE
```

#### TAIL ROTOR FAILURE / LOSS OF TAIL ROTOR THRUST — FORWARD FLIGHT
```
1. AUTOROTATE .............. Enter autorotation
2. AIRSPEED ................ MAINTAIN above ETL (vertical fin provides stability)
3. THROTTLE ................ CLOSE for landing
4. EXECUTE RUN-ON LANDING .. Do not attempt to hover
```

#### MAST BUMPING — LOW-G RECOVERY
```
1. AFT CYCLIC .............. GENTLY apply (reload the rotor disc)
2. DO NOT PUSH FORWARD ..... NEVER apply forward cyclic under low-G
3. COLLECTIVE .............. AS REQUIRED to reload disc
4. IF IMPACT HAS OCCURRED .. No recovery — mast separation is catastrophic
```

## Part 2 — Engine Failure / Autorotation Training

### Autorotation Physics (UH-1H Specific)

The UH-1H two-blade teetering rotor has **less rotational inertia** than multi-blade systems. This is the single most important characteristic affecting autorotation:

| Factor | UH-1H Two-Blade | Multi-Blade Comparison |
|---|---|---|
| Nr decay rate after power loss | Fast — drops below 294 RPM within **~1–2 seconds** without collective reduction | ~3–4 seconds margin |
| Collective reaction requirement | **Immediate** — must lower within 1 second | Slightly more margin |
| Flare energy available | Less stored energy; timing is critical | More forgiving |
| Collective pull timing | One pull, one chance; early or late = hard landing | Slightly more margin |

> **Training mantra:** "Engine failure → collective DOWN is a REFLEX. Not a thought. Not a decision. A reflex."

### Autorotation Phases and Technique

| Phase | Altitude (AGL) | Airspeed | Collective | Pedals | Cyclic |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Entry** | Any | Transitioning to 65 KIAS | **DOWN — immediately** | Right pedal (no torque) | Forward to 65 KIAS |
| **2. Glide** | Descending | 65 KIAS | Regulate Nr 294–324 | Heading maintenance | Hold 65 KIAS attitude |
| **3. Select LZ** | >300 ft AGL | 65 KIAS | As glide | As glide | As glide |
| **4. Flare** | ~75–100 ft AGL | Decelerating from 65 | No change yet — wait for level | Heading maintenance | **Aft cyclic ~20–30°** — raises nose, decelerates, builds Nr |
| **5. Level** | ~15–20 ft AGL | ~20–30 KIAS | Begin collective pull | Heading | **Forward to level attitude** |
| **6. Pull** | ~10–15 ft AGL | ~10–20 KIAS | **Pull firmly** — arrest rate of descent | Heading | Hold level; slight nose-up attitude |
| **7. Touchdown** | Ground | ~0–10 KIAS | Continue lowering as aircraft settles | Maintain heading | Hold level; slight nose-up |

### Training Sequence (In Order)

Students complete autorotations in the following order. Each step must be completed before advancing.

| Stage | Description | Entry Condition | Termination |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A1 — Demo** | IP demonstrates straight-in auto with power recovery | 1,500 ft AGL, 65 KIAS | Power recovery at 75 ft AGL |
| **A2 — Power recovery (student)** | Student performs entry and glide; IP cues; power recovery | 1,500 ft AGL | Power recovery at 75 ft AGL |
| **A3 — Student, unaided** | Student performs full sequence; IP does not assist unless unsafe | 1,500 ft AGL | Power recovery at 75 ft AGL |
| **A4 — Surprise entry** | IP introduces failure without warning during cruise | Cruise, ~80 KIAS | Power recovery |
| **A5 — Hover failure** | IP introduces failure while in a 5 ft hover | 5 ft hover | Skid contact (simulated — use collective cushion only; no damage) |
| **A6 — 90° profile** | Enter auto; make 90° turn to align with designated spot | 1,500 ft AGL | Power recovery or full touchdown at IP discretion |
| **A7 — 180° profile** | Enter auto; execute 180° turn; land on original heading zone | 1,500 ft AGL | Power recovery or full touchdown |
| **A8 — Full touchdown** | Complete autorotation from entry to skid contact | 1,500 ft AGL, flat surface | Touchdown; no damage |

### DCS Keybind Reference for Autorotation

| Action | DCS Control |
|---|---|
| Lower collective (entry) | `PgDn` (rapid input at entry) |
| Close throttle | Mouse wheel on throttle grip in cockpit (full counterclockwise) |
| Establish airspeed | Cyclic forward/aft (axis or `W`/`S` keys) |
| Maintain heading | Pedals (axis or `Z`/`X` keys) |
| Flare | Aft cyclic pull |
| Collective pull (cushion) | `PgUp` (firm, progressive) |

### Auto Entry — Critical Timing Drill

The IP will call "engine failure" at various conditions. Student response is timed and judged:

| Response Time | Grade |
|---|---|
| Collective down within **1 second** | PASS |
| Collective down within 2 seconds | MARGINAL — note; brief on urgency |
| Collective down after 3+ seconds | FAIL — repeat drill; Nr likely below limits |

## Part 3 — Engine Fire in Flight

### Recognition
- ENGINE FIRE light illuminates on caution panel.
- Smell of burning or smoke entering the cockpit.
- In DCS: caution light and possible external smoke effect visible.

### Procedure

| Step | Action | DCS Input |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Throttle — CLOSE** (enter autorotation) | Mouse wheel on throttle grip to OFF position |
| 2 | **Emergency fuel shutoff — OFF** | Fuel valve switch `LShift + F` → OFF |
| 3 | **Cabin Heat — OFF** | Cockpit cabin heat switch (if simulated) |
| 4 | **Autorotate to nearest suitable area** | Full autorotation technique as above |
| 5 | After landing: **Battery OFF** | `LShift + B` → OFF |
| 6 | **Egress immediately** | Leave the aircraft away from the direction of the fire |

> **Speed is critical:** Engine fire with fuel flowing will progress rapidly. The difference between fire containment and aircraft loss may be seconds. The throttle and fuel shutoff must be immediate and simultaneous with the autorotation entry.

## Part 4 — Tail Rotor Failure / LTE

### Tail Rotor Failure — Forward Flight (Above ETL)

**Symptoms:**
- Aircraft begins to yaw right (clockwise from above) uncontrollably.
- Full left pedal does not stop the yaw.
- Control feedback through pedals changes.

| Step | Action | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Recognize** — uncommanded right yaw; full left pedal ineffective | Do not add more left pedal compulsively — diagnose first |
| 2 | **Autorotate** — lower collective, close throttle | Removes engine torque; yaw stops when torque is zero |
| 3 | **Maintain airspeed above ETL** | Vertical fin provides directional stability above ~24 KIAS |
| 4 | **Execute run-on landing** | Do NOT attempt to hover — zero forward speed = no fin stabilization = spinning |

### Loss of Tail Rotor Effectiveness (LTE)

LTE is an aerodynamic degradation of anti-torque capability caused by specific wind conditions — **the tail rotor is physically intact** but aerodynamically ineffective.

#### LTE Trigger Wind Azimuths

| Wind From (Relative to Nose) | Azimuth | Mechanism | Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Left rear quarter** | 285°–315° | Main rotor downwash vortex blown into tail rotor disc | High |
| **Tail (rear sector)** | 120°–240° | Weathercock effect — wind pushes the tail downwind, nose yaws right | High |
| **Left rear arc (tail rotor VRS)** | 210°–330° | Tail rotor enters vortex ring state — thrustcollapses suddenly | Very High |

#### LTE Recognition

- Uncommanded right yaw (nose swings right without pedal input).
- Full left pedal applied but yaw continues or accelerates.
- May be accompanied by tail buffeting.

#### LTE Recovery

| Step | Action | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Forward cyclic — immediately** | Gain airspeed; 15–20 KIAS makes vertical fin effective |
| 2 | **Lower collective** — if altitude permits | Reduces torque; reduces tail rotor demand |
| 3 | **Do not add more left pedal** if tail rotor is in vortex ring state | More pedal input can worsen the VRS of the tail rotor |
| 4 | If spin develops below 200 ft AGL | **Autorotate** — lower collective removes torque entirely; yaw stops |

**Prevention:**
- Avoid hovering with wind from the 210°–330° azimuth (relative to nose).
- Keep pedal turns slow and deliberate.
- Accelerate to forward flight if wind direction becomes dangerous.
- Maintain constant wind-azimuth awareness during all hover and low-speed operations.

### DCS LTE Training Drill

1. Establish a hover at 50 ft AGL with ~15-knot wind from the left-rear azimuth (set in mission editor).
2. Perform slow right pedal turns to bring the wind into the LTE azimuth.
3. Observe onset of uncommanded yaw.
4. Apply recovery: forward cyclic, reduce collective.
5. Allow student to experience this at altitude (1,000 ft AGL) intentionally before attempting recovery at low altitude.

## Part 5 — Settling with Power (VRS)

### Entry Conditions

All three conditions must be present simultaneously:

| Condition | Threshold |
|---|---|
| **Airspeed** | Below ~30 KIAS |
| **Rate of descent** | Exceeding ~300 fpm |
| **Power setting** | Moderate to high (collective raised, engine torquing) |

### Recognition

- High rate of descent (1,000–3,000+ fpm) despite significant power application.
- Aircraft shuddering and vibrating irregularly.
- Increasing collective does **not** arrest the descent — may worsen it.
- Possible random pitch and roll excursions.

### Recovery

| Step | Action | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Forward cyclic — aggressively** | Gain airspeed to fly out of the vortex; rotor moves into clean air |
| 2 | **Lower collective momentarily** | Reduces downwash that feeds the vortex ring |
| 3 | **Gain airspeed above 30 KIAS** | Once above 30 KIAS, rotor disc is in clean air; normal flight resumes |
| 4 | **Apply power normally once clear** | Climb away |

> **Critical:** VRS recovery requires **200–500 ft of altitude**. If VRS develops below 500 ft AGL, ground impact is likely before recovery is complete. Prevention is the only reliable protection below 500 ft AGL.

**Prevention:**
- Never allow descent rate >300 fpm below 30 KIAS.
- On steep approaches, maintain >30–40 KIAS until the final deceleration to hover.
- If a slow, steep descent is unavoidable, maintain 10–15 knots of lateral or forward movement to break the VRS pattern.

### DCS VRS Training Drill

1. Climb to 2,000 ft AGL.
2. Decelerate to hover.
3. Establish a controlled descent — raise collective to moderate (30+ PSI torque) while descending at ~500 fpm with near-zero airspeed.
4. Observe VRS onset — aircraft sinks, vibrates, does not respond to collective.
5. Apply forward cyclic immediately; lower collective briefly.
6. Exit VRS and recover to level flight.
7. Brief the student: *"This is how it starts. At 200 ft AGL, you don't get to try recovery."*

## Part 6 — Governor Failure / Manual Throttle

### Recognition

- Nr fluctuates beyond normal governor range (beyond ±3 RPM).
- GOV EMER caution light may illuminate.
- Nr trends high or low with collective changes without automatic correction.

### Immediate Action

| Step | Action | DCS Input |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **GOV switch — OFF** | Cockpit governor switch (`LCtrl + Home`) |
| 2 | **Throttle — MANUAL** | Mouse wheel on throttle grip in cockpit |
| 3 | Adjust throttle to maintain **Nr 324 RPM** | Twist grip right (clockwise) to add fuel; left (counterclockwise) to reduce |
| 4 | **Coordinate collective and throttle** | Collective up → add throttle; collective down → reduce throttle |
| 5 | **Land as soon as practicable** | Plan approach immediately |

### Manual Throttle Technique

| Control Input | Effect on Nr | Throttle Correction |
|---|---|---|
| **Collective UP** | Nr drops (more rotor demand) | Twist throttle clockwise (add fuel) |
| **Collective DOWN** | Nr rises (less rotor demand) | Twist throttle counterclockwise (reduce fuel) |
| **Gust / turbulence** | Nr varies | Constant small throttle adjustments |
| **Climbing** | Nr drops | Add throttle |
| **Descending** | Nr rises | Reduce throttle |

> **Priority:** Nr management above all. Sacrifice altitude and heading precision to maintain Nr within limits. Nr outside 294–345 RPM = emergency within an emergency.

### Manual Throttle Practice Drill

1. In level flight at altitude, turn GOV switch OFF (simulates failure).
2. Student maintains Nr 324 RPM with throttle only.
3. IP calls for collective changes (climb, descent, deceleration, acceleration).
4. Student must keep Nr within ±5 RPM of 324 throughout.
5. Student plans and executes a full approach to landing with manual throttle.

## Part 7 — Hydraulic Failure

### Recognition

- Flight controls suddenly become **extremely heavy** — abrupt increase in control forces.
- HYDRAULIC PRESS caution light illuminates.
- Hydraulic pressure gauge drops to zero.
- Master caution may illuminate.

### Flight Characteristics Without Hydraulics

| Control | Force Without Hydraulics |
|---|---|
| **Cyclic** | 40–60+ lbs depending on airspeed and maneuver |
| **Collective** | Heavy but manageable |
| **Pedals** | Very heavy |
| **Best speed for manageable forces** | **60–80 KIAS** — lighter forces than slow or very fast flight |

### Procedure

| Step | Action | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Recognize** heavy controls; check hydraulic pressure caution | Do not over-correct — inputs require more force |
| 2 | **Adjust airspeed to 60–80 KIAS** | Most manageable control range |
| 3 | **Plan a run-on landing** at nearest suitable field | Avoid hover — constant small corrections in a hover without hydraulics are exhausting and imprecise |
| 4 | **HYD CONTROL switch — OFF** if controls surge or uncommanded | Eliminates remaining hydraulic pressure from causing erratic inputs |
| 5 | **Shallow approach** — 3–6° angle | Maintain translational lift to touchdown |
| 6 | **Touch down at 20–30 KIAS forward speed** | Slide to a stop on skids |
| 7 | **Normal shutdown after stop** | Do not attempt to reposition without hydraulics |

> **DCS Note:** Hydraulic failure is modeled in DCS with increased control forces. Force-feedback sticks provide a better approximation of the real aircraft. With a standard spring joystick, the DCS model conveys the concept but not the actual physical fatigue of the real aircraft.

## Part 8 — Electrical Failure Management

### Generator Failure

| Step | Action | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | GEN caution light illuminates; voltmeter drops | Battery now sole power source |
| 2 | **GEN switch — RESET, then ON** | May restore generator; attempt once |
| 3 | If no restoration: **shed non-essential loads** | Radios (keep one), non-essential lights, unnecessary equipment |
| 4 | **Battery endurance: ~30 minutes** at minimum load | Plan approach before battery dies |
| 5 | **Land as soon as practicable** | Priority: one comm radio, flight instruments, caution panel |

### Essential vs. Non-Essential Loads

| Essential (keep) | Non-Essential (shed first) |
|---|---|
| One communication radio | Non-essential radios |
| Flight instruments (AI, DG via inverter) | Cargo/cabin lighting |
| Engine instruments (most mechanical — no power needed) | Non-essential navigation lights |
| Caution panel | Equipment not needed for the emergency |

### Total Electrical Failure (Battery Also Dead)

- All electrically-powered instruments lost (AI, DG, radios, caution panel).
- Engine continues — fuel control does not require electricity.
- Navigate by magnetic compass and outside visual reference.
- No radio communication.
- Fly to nearest airfield; execute visual approach.
- Squawk 7600 (radio failure) on transponder if it was set before battery died.

## Part 9 — Transmission and Fuel System Malfunctions

### Transmission Failure Indications

| Indication | Severity | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **CHIP DET light** | Metal particles — early warning | **Land as soon as possible** — find nearest safe landing area now |
| **XMSN OIL PRESS low** | Loss of lubrication | **Land immediately** — transmission may seize without oil |
| **XMSN OIL TEMP >120°C** | Overheating | Reduce power; **land immediately** |
| **Unusual noise/vibration** | Possible gear or bearing failure | Reduce power; **land immediately** |

### "As Soon As Possible" vs. "As Soon As Practicable"

| Phrase | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **Land as soon as possible** | Land **NOW** at the nearest point where a safe landing can be made — a field, road, clearing, anywhere safe. The situation is critical and immediate. |
| **Land as soon as practicable** | Land at the nearest suitable airfield or safe area without unnecessarily extending flight. The situation is serious but not immediately critical. |

### Fuel System Malfunctions

| Condition | Indication | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Fuel boost pump failure** | FUEL BOOST caution light | Land as soon as practicable; maintain level flight for gravity feed |
| **Fuel filter bypass** | FUEL FILTER caution light | Land as soon as practicable — unfiltered fuel reaching engine |
| **Fuel starvation** | EGT drops, N1 drops, Nr decreases; fuel quantity shows remaining fuel | Autorotate; check fuel valve, boost pump, fuel selector |
| **Fuel exhaustion** | Same as starvation; fuel quantity at/near zero | Autorotate immediately — no recovery possible |

## Part 10 — Mast Bumping and Dynamic Rollover Review

### Mast Bumping

**The UH-1H teetering rotor is the most feared failure mode because recovery is impossible once mast contact occurs.**

**Prevention — the ONLY effective response:**

| Rule | Action |
|---|---|
| **Never push forward cyclic aggressively** | Period. No exceptions. Any condition. |
| **Maintain positive G at all times** | Never allow a zero-G or negative-G flight state |
| **In turbulence** | Slow down; accept a rough ride; do NOT use cyclic to smooth it |
| **After engine failure** | Smooth entry — do NOT push the nose over to accelerate |
| **Light gross weight** | Increased susceptibility — more caution required |

**Low-G Recovery (if you recognize the condition before mast contact):**

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | **Aft cyclic — gently** (reload the rotor disc) |
| 2 | **NEVER apply forward cyclic under low-G** |
| 3 | **Collective as required** to reload disc |

> **Reality check:** If mast contact has occurred, mast separation is catastrophic and there is no recovery. The rotor departs the aircraft. Prevention is not just the best response — it is the *only* response. Make this clear to every student at every opportunity.

### Dynamic Rollover

**Conditions that create dynamic rollover risk:**

- One skid on the ground (slope operations, uneven terrain).
- Lateral drift at touchdown or liftoff.
- Crosswind pushing the aircraft laterally.
- External connection not removed before liftoff.

**Critical Roll Rate:**
Once roll rate exceeds ~10°/second around the ground contact point, cyclic authority alone is insufficient to stop the roll.

**Emergency Procedure:**

| Condition | Action |
|---|---|
| Roll rate low (<10°/sec), early in the roll | **Lower collective** — plant both skids on the ground immediately |
| Roll rate high (>10°/sec) | Collective DOWN + full opposite cyclic simultaneously — outcome uncertain; may not arrest the roll |

> **The lesson:** Dynamic rollover is prevented, not recovered from. Slow movements on slopes, smooth liftoffs in crosswind, and verifying no tiedowns before liftoff are the only reliable countermeasures.

## DCS Emergency Modeling Summary

| Emergency | DCS Models It? | Fidelity | Training Value |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Engine failure** | Yes | High | Full autorotation training — primary EP skill |
| **Engine fire** | Yes | Moderate | Fire warning; shutdown procedure teachable |
| **Governor failure** | Yes | High | Manual throttle required; excellent practice |
| **Hydraulic failure** | Yes | High | Heavy controls; run-on landing required |
| **Tail rotor failure** | Yes | High | Loss of anti-torque; autorotation to run-on required |
| **LTE** | Yes | High | Wind-azimuth modeled; intentional LTE drill feasible |
| **VRS / Settling with power** | Yes | High | Onset symptoms and recovery representative |
| **Mast bumping** | Yes | High | Can destroy aircraft; low-G scenario trainable |
| **Dynamic rollover** | Yes | Moderate | Slope operations; slower buildup than real aircraft |
| **Generator failure** | Yes | Moderate | Electrical load-shedding trainable |
| **Fuel starvation/exhaustion** | Yes | Moderate | Engine quits at fuel exhaustion |
| **Transmission failure (chip detector)** | Partial | Low | Caution light; limited failure progression |
| **Compressor stall** | No | N/A | Academic knowledge only — not trainable in DCS |

### Setting DCS Failures for EP Training

Use the **Mission Editor → Failure System** to configure:
- Engine failure: set to trigger at a specific time (e.g., T+5 min) or randomly within a window.
- Hydraulic failure: set for activation during cruise to force a planning decision.
- Governor failure: set to trigger during pattern flight.
- For autorotation from hover: manually close the throttle in cockpit during the event to simulate engine failure at hover.

| Standard | Requirement |
|---|---|

## Common Errors

| Error | Consequence | Correction |

| Delayed collective reduction on engine failure | Nr drops below 294; landing hard; possible aircraft damage | Drill until reflexive — 2 second max response. Quiz every brief |
