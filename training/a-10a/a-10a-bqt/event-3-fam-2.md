## Learning Objectives

Upon completion of this event the student will be able to:
- [ ] Configure the aircraft for slow flight with MVR and DN flaps; maintain controlled flight to buffet onset
- [ ] Recognize approach-to-stall onset using AOA indexer, buffet, and aural tone
- [ ] Execute a prompt stall recovery maintaining altitude loss < 500 ft with no secondary stall
- [ ] Perform 45° and 60° banked turns maintaining altitude within standards
- [ ] Demonstrate correct speed brake technique for descent management
- [ ] Demonstrate proper trim technique during all configuration changes

## Ground Brief

### Mission Profile
This flight focuses entirely on airwork. No new ground procedures are introduced — startup and taxi are now the student's responsibility from memory.

1. **Phase 1:** Departure and climb to working altitude
2. **Phase 2:** Slow flight — approach and maintain minimum controlled airspeed
3. **Phase 3:** Approach-to-stall and recovery — wings-level and turning
4. **Phase 4:** Steep turns — 45° and 60°
5. **Phase 5:** Speed brake technique for energy management
6. **Phase 6:** Return and landing (two approaches minimum — one touch-and-go, one full stop)

### Key Numbers for This Flight

| Item | Value |
|---|---|
| Fuel Load | 50% internal |
| Stores | Clean |
| Working Altitude | 10,000 ft MSL (minimum 5,000 ft AGL recovery floor) |
| Slow Flight Speed | 140–150 KIAS (MVR flaps); 120–130 KIAS (DN flaps) |
| Stall Warning (AOA) | ~20 units (amber chevron up + tone) |
| Full Stall | ~23 AoA units (~125–130 KIAS clean) |
| Minimum Recovery Altitude | 5,000 ft AGL above terrain |

### Stall Recovery Brief
The A-10A stall is benign but requires prompt action:
1. **Nose down** — reduce AoA; do NOT pull
2. **Wings level** — maintain coordinated flight
3. **Power up** — full military power
4. **Recover** — level off with minimum altitude loss

If in a **turning stall**, roll wings level first, then lower the nose. Do not apply rudder to oppose the roll — use aileron.

## Part 1 — Departure & Area Transit

### Startup & Taxi (Student Memory Check)
The student performs the full cold-start and taxi sequence without reference to notes. Instructor evaluates:
- [ ] EGT managed below 649°C on both starts
- [ ] Correct taxi speed and NWS technique
- [ ] Pre-takeoff checks performed at hold-short

### Departure
- [ ] Normal takeoff as practiced in Event 2
- [ ] Clean-up on schedule (gear up positive rate; flaps up above 150 KIAS + 200 ft AGL)
- [ ] Climb to **10,000 ft MSL** at Vy (200–210 KIAS)
- [ ] Level off; trim to hands-off cruise

## Part 2 — Slow Flight

Slow flight explores the aircraft's handling at the low end of the flight envelope. The goal is to understand how the controls feel and how the AOA indexer guides you at critically low speeds.

### Slow Flight Setup
- [ ] Establish level flight at 10,000 ft MSL; reduce throttle to 55–60% N2
- [ ] Allow speed to decrease through 200 KIAS
- [ ] At 200 KIAS: **Flaps MVR** (`LShift + F` cycle to MVR)
- [ ] Trim nose slightly up; maintain altitude
- [ ] Continue reducing power; allow speed to decrease to **140–150 KIAS**

### Slow Flight with MVR Flaps
- [ ] Maintain altitude ± 100 ft at 140–150 KIAS
- [ ] Observe: aircraft is controllable but sluggish; controls feel heavier
- [ ] Keep AOA indexer in the amber-donut transition zone (slightly slow is acceptable)
- [ ] Practice gentle turns: 15–20° bank only; note increased altitude loss tendency

### Slow Flight with DN Flaps
- [ ] Further reduce power; extend **Flaps DN** (`LCtrl + F`)
- [ ] Allow speed to decrease to **120–130 KIAS**
- [ ] Maintain altitude ± 150 ft
- [ ] This approximates the final approach configuration — practice holding the green donut

**Instructor Note:** Many students never get comfortable at slow speeds because they "chase" airspeed with large throttle inputs. Teach them to fly AoA and let speed settle. The green donut is your friend.

### Recovery from Slow Flight
- [ ] Simultaneously: **Full power** + **Flaps MVR**
- [ ] Maintain altitude (add back pressure as needed)
- [ ] When above 160 KIAS: **Flaps UP**
- [ ] Trim for cruise; accelerate to normal cruise speed

## Part 3 — Approach-to-Stall & Recovery

> **Safety:** Minimum entry altitude for stall practice: **10,000 ft MSL**. Minimum recovery altitude: **5,000 ft MSL** (do not allow altitude to decrease below this during any stall maneuver). If altitude reaches 6,000 ft MSL during recovery, stop and climb before continuing.

### A: Wings-Level Stall

1. **Entry:** Level flight, clean configuration. Reduce power to idle.
2. **Deceleration:** Maintain level flight with back pressure as speed decreases.
3. **Indications to recognize:**
   - [ ] Amber chevron UP on AOA indexer
   - [ ] Airframe buffet (light shudder)
   - [ ] Aural AoA tone increasing in pitch
4. **Stall:** At ~20 AoA units / ~125–130 KIAS (clean) — nose drops; may have slight wing rock
5. **Recovery:**
   - [ ] **Nose DOWN** — break the stall; lower pitch 5–10°
   - [ ] **Wings LEVEL** — counter any roll with aileron
   - [ ] **Full POWER** — throttles to military
   - [ ] **Recover** — pull smoothly out of dive; no secondary stall
6. **Standard:** Altitude loss < 500 ft; no secondary stall; wings level throughout recovery

**Repeat 3 times in each direction before proceeding.**

### B: Turning Stall (30° Bank)

1. Establish a 30° banked turn; reduce power to 50% N2
2. Hold altitude with back pressure; allow speed to decay
3. Observe: stall warning occurs sooner in a turn (higher G-load increases stall speed)
4. At first buffet or amber chevron: immediately begin recovery
5. **Recovery:** Simultaneously — reduce back pressure + roll wings level + add power
6. **Standard:** Altitude loss < 500 ft; no spin departure

**Repeat in both directions.**

## Part 4 — Steep Turns

Steep turns demonstrate load factor management and precise aircraft control at higher G.

### 45° Banked Turn

| Item | Standard |
|---|---|
| Bank angle | 45° ± 5° |
| Entry and exit heading | ±10° |
| Altitude | ±150 ft throughout |
| AOA | Below stall warning throughout |

**Technique:**
- [ ] Establish 250 KIAS, level flight
- [ ] Roll into 45° bank; apply back pressure to maintain altitude (expect ~1.4 G)
- [ ] Trim if desired — some pilots prefer to hold the turn, others trim for it
- [ ] Complete 360° turn; roll out on entry heading
- [ ] Repeat in the opposite direction

### 60° Banked Turn (Demonstration)

The 60° turn is primarily a demonstration of load factor effects — this bank requires 2.0 G to maintain altitude.
- [ ] Roll to 60°; apply back pressure — feel the G-load
- [ ] Observe: AOA increases quickly; speed bleeds fast
- [ ] Complete 180° only; do not sustain — return to level flight
- [ ] **Do NOT stall in a 60° banked turn** — minimum airspeed: 200 KIAS entry

**Instructor Note:** The purpose of the 60° demo is to feel the energy bleed. Students who are surprised by A-10A energy management in combat often failed to internalize this lesson.

## Part 5 — Speed Brake Usage & Descent Technique

The split speed brakes on the A-10A are highly effective and introduce minimal pitch change. Teaching students to use them aggressively for descent management prevents the bad habit of nosing over and building speed.

### Speed Brake Demonstration
- [ ] At cruise altitude and 280 KIAS, extend **speed brakes fully** (`LShift + B` or `B`)
- [ ] Observe deceleration: ~20–30 KIAS per minute
- [ ] Note: minimal pitch change (slight nose-up tendency — easily trimmed)
- [ ] Close speed brakes (`LCtrl + B` or `B` toggle); observe acceleration

### Descent with Speed Brakes
- [ ] Reduce power to 60% N2; extend speed brakes fully
- [ ] Establish 2,000 ft/min descent; maintain 260–280 KIAS
- [ ] Compare to descent without speed brakes — same rate requires much steeper nose-down attitude
- [ ] **Lesson:** Use speed brakes to descend steeply while maintaining a nose-level attitude (better situational awareness and airfield awareness)

## Part 6 — Return & Landing

This event requires a minimum of **two approaches**:
1. **Touch-and-go** — full landing, then immediately apply full power + flaps MVR + rotate again
2. **Full stop** — final approach with complete rollout

### Touch-and-Go Procedure
1. Fly standard pattern as practiced in Event 2
2. Touchdown normally — main gear then nosewheel
3. Immediately: **Throttles MAX** + **Flaps MVR** (`LShift + F`)
4. Accelerate to Vr (~125 KIAS) → rotate → depart
5. Clean up as normal (gear up, flaps up on schedule)

**Touch-and-Go Standard:** Full power within 3 sec of touchdown; rotation within 1,500 ft of touchdown point.

| Speed brake use | Demonstrates effective descent profile with SBs |

| Full-stop landing | AOA donut ± 1 unit on final; touchdown within first 1,000 ft |

## Common Errors

| Error | Correction |
|---|---|
| Adding power before lowering nose during stall recovery | Nose DOWN first — power is the second action |
| Pulling back when stall occurs in a turn | Roll wings level simultaneously with nose-down; rudder is not the fix |
| Not trimming during slow flight | Each flap extension requires re-trim; hands-off should be achievable |
| Over-controlling in steep turns | Small, smooth inputs; let the aircraft settle into the bank angle |
| Not using speed brakes during descents | Brief students: speed brakes are a gear of the A-10; use them proactively |
| Rushing the touch-and-go power application | Clear the landing attitude first, then power; premature pull causes porpoise |
