## Learning Objectives

Upon completion of this event the student will be able to:
- [ ] Fly a standard VFR helicopter traffic pattern at correct altitudes and speeds
- [ ] Execute three normal decelerating approaches to a consistent termination point
- [ ] Execute a steep approach (14–20°) to a confined landing area
- [ ] Execute a shallow approach / running landing at 20–30 KIAS touchdown
- [ ] Execute a go-around from any phase of an approach
- [ ] Describe slope landing technique and execute a slope landing within limits (≤ 10° slope)
- [ ] Brief a confined area approach using the recon orbit technique

## Part 2 — Standard VFR Helicopter Pattern

The helicopter traffic pattern differs from the fixed-wing pattern in altitude, speed, and approach geometry. The helicopter pattern is smaller and flown at lower altitude to permit the required steep glidepaths.

### Pattern Geometry

```
                    CROSSWIND
                    (500 ft AGL)
                   ____________
                  |            |
   UPWIND         |            |  DOWNWIND
   Climbing out   |  ABEAM     |  70–80 KIAS
   after takeoff  |  POINT     |  700 ft AGL
                  |____________|
                       FINAL
                    8–12° glidepath
                    toward landing point
```

### Standard Pattern Legs

| Leg | Airspeed | Altitude | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Upwind / Departure** | 50–70 KIAS climbing | 300 ft AGL → 700 ft AGL | Climb to pattern altitude on runway heading |
| **Crosswind** | 70 KIAS | 500–700 ft AGL | Turn 90° crosswind; continue climbing |
| **Downwind** | 70–80 KIAS | 700 ft AGL | Parallel to runway/pad; abeam point is key |
| **Abeam Point** | 70–80 KIAS | 700 ft AGL | Landing point is at your 3 or 9 o'clock; begin deceleration |
| **Base** | 50–60 KIAS | 400–500 ft AGL | 90° turn toward final; continue decelerating |
| **Final** | 40–50 KIAS → 0 | 300 ft AGL → 0 | Established on glidepath; decelerate to hover |

**Abeam Cue:** When the landing pad is directly off the side of the aircraft, that is the "abeam point." This is when you:
1. Begin descent (reduce collective slightly)
2. Begin deceleration (slight aft cyclic)
3. Begin the base turn

**Base Turn Timing:** Begin the base turn when the landing point is at approximately 45° behind the aircraft (looking over the shoulder). Too early = overshooting final. Too late = wide base and long approach.

## Part 3 — Normal Approach (Multiple Reps)

The student will fly the traffic pattern and execute **three consecutive normal approaches** to develop consistency.

### Normal Approach Technique (Expanded)

#### Setting Up Final

On final, establish the following at 300–400 ft AGL:
- **Airspeed:** 40–50 KIAS
- **Glidepath:** 8–12° (visual — landing point should be at a fixed position in the windscreen, neither rising nor falling)
- **Heading:** on centerline

**The visual glidepath:** If the landing point is **rising in your windscreen**, you are above glidepath (too high). If it is **falling**, you are below glidepath (too low). A point that remains fixed in the windscreen means you are on a collision course — which is what you want for an approach.

#### Through 200 ft AGL

- Airspeed: 20–30 KIAS
- Begin the **power reduction and cyclic back** to arrest forward velocity
- The aircraft is exiting ETL — expect a change in power requirement

#### Through 50 ft AGL

- Airspeed: 10–15 KIAS
- Flare (aft cyclic) to arrest remaining forward velocity
- Collective to arrest the descent rate
- These two inputs happen simultaneously and must be coordinated

#### Termination

- Arrive at a stable hover (3–5 ft) over the designated landing point
- Once stable, lower collective to set down

**Consistency Check:** After 3 approaches, the student should be able to state:
- Am I consistently arriving at the landing point or overshooting/undershooting?
- Am I arriving too fast? Too slow? Adjustments needed?

## Part 4 — Steep Approach

The steep approach (14–20°) is used for confined areas, pinnacles, and areas with obstacles on the approach path.

### When to Use a Steep Approach

| Situation | Why Steep |
|---|---|
| Obstacles on approach path | Higher angle clears wires, trees, ridgelines |
| Confined landing zone | Steep angle allows precise touchdown without overflying obstacles |
| Pinnacle landing | Must descend steeply to reach the peak |
| Short final field | Steep angle maximizes glide into small areas |

### Steep Approach Technique

| Phase | Airspeed | Angle | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Final entry** | 30–40 KIAS | 14–20° | Steeper than normal; nose lower attitude |
| **Mid-final** | 20–30 KIAS | Maintained | Vertical velocity higher than normal — monitor closely |
| **Short final** | 10–15 KIAS | Reducing | Begin aft cyclic; collective to arrest rate |
| **Termination** | Hover 3–5 ft | — | Over landing point |

**Critical Difference from Normal Approach:**
- Higher rate of descent throughout
- More collective required to arrest the final descent
- Slower deceleration timing — must start slowing LATER than a normal approach
- SWP risk is elevated if descent rate becomes excessive with power applied

**SWP Avoidance:** If the descent rate becomes uncomfortable and you have power applied, you are approaching SWP conditions. The correction is **cyclic forward** to gain airspeed and break out of the vortex state.

> *⚠ The steep approach is where most settling-with-power accidents occur. The student must maintain situational awareness of airspeed and descent rate throughout.*

## Part 5 — Shallow Approach / Running Landing

The shallow approach (4–7°) and running landing are used when:
- The landing area is too small for a hover but sufficient for a run-on
- The aircraft is at or near maximum gross weight (hover OGE not achievable)
- Hydraulic failure — run-on landing preferred to hover

### Shallow / Running Landing Technique

| Phase | Airspeed | Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Final entry** | 50–60 KIAS | Shallower glidepath than normal |
| **Mid-final** | 40–50 KIAS | Maintain shallow angle; do not decelerate below 30 KIAS before 20 ft |
| **Ground effect zone** | 30–40 KIAS | Aircraft enters IGE — float tendency; reduce collective slightly |
| **Touchdown** | 20–30 KIAS | Allow skids to contact ground while still moving forward at 20–30 KIAS |
| **Ground roll** | Decelerating | After skid contact, allow aircraft to decelerate on the surface |

**Running landing touchdown technique:**
1. Skids contact the ground while the aircraft is moving forward
2. Do NOT pull aft cyclic aggressively — this can cause the tail to strike
3. Allow the aircraft to decelerate naturally; pedals for directional control
4. Once stopped, lower collective; engine at idle; aircraft is parked

## Part 6 — Go-Around

A go-around is initiated any time the approach becomes unsafe or parameters are exceeded. The student must be prepared to execute a go-around at **any point on final** — at the instructor's command or on their own initiative.

### Go-Around Conditions

| Situation | Response |
|---|---|
| **Too fast / too high on final** | Go-around; do not try to salvage a bad approach |
| **Landing area obstructed** | Go-around |
| **Instructor command: "Go-around"** | Immediate execution |
| **Any doubt about safety** | Go-around — always the right decision |

### Go-Around Procedure

| Step | Action |
|---|---|
| 1 | **Collective** — **INCREASE** (apply climb power) |
| 2 | **Cyclic** — **FORWARD** (gain airspeed; build through ETL) |
| 3 | **Pedal** — **LEFT** as torque increases |
| 4 | At **30+ KIAS** — establish climb attitude |
| 5 | Climb to pattern altitude; re-enter traffic pattern |

**Key Principle:** A go-around must be initiated and committed to immediately. Hovering minds and indecision on short final is dangerous. At < 50 ft AGL with high rate of descent, there may not be time to go around — this is why we practice early initiation.

**Lowest Safe Go-Around:** The lowest practical go-around is approximately 20–30 ft AGL with sufficient forward cyclic input to accelerate through ETL before touchdown. Below this, a controlled crash is preferable to a go-around that cannot be completed.

## Part 7 — Slope Landing

Slope landings are among the highest-risk phases of helicopter operations. Dynamic rollover is the primary hazard.

### Slope Landing Technique

**Approach:** Use a normal approach to arrive at a hover 3–5 ft over the slope landing point.

**Touchdown Sequence:**

| Step | Action | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | From hover, position aircraft directly over slope | Lateral displacement from the slope increases rollover risk |
| 2 | **Upslope skid FIRST** — lower it to the slope | Reduces lateral roll moment on initial contact |
| 3 | Lower collective **slowly** — aircraft settles onto upslope skid | Do not allow a rapid collective drop |
| 4 | **Do NOT apply lateral cyclic toward the downslope side** | This adds to the rollover moment — will cause dynamic rollover |
| 5 | As downslope skid approaches the ground, lower collective further | Both skids now near contact |
| 6 | Both skids contact — **continue lowering collective to full down** | Removing all lift removes the rolling tendency |

**Dynamic Rollover Avoidance:**
- Any time one skid is on the ground and the other is in the air, a rolling moment exists
- If the roll rate exceeds ~10°/sec toward the downslope, and the cyclic is at its stop → unrecoverable rollover
- **Prevention:** Lower collective aggressively the moment rollover tendency is felt

**Slope Limits:**

| Slope Type | Limit |
|---|---|
| Side slope (lateral) | ≤ 10° |
| Nose-up | ≤ 10° |
| Nose-down | ≤ 5° |

## Part 8 — Confined Area Approach (Introduction)

This section introduces the concept of confined area operations without full execution. The student will brief the approach but may fly it at the instructor's discretion.

### Confined Area Definition

A **confined area** is any landing site where the size, shape, or surrounding obstacles restrict the flight path or landing approach. Confined areas require the aircraft to:
1. Clear obstacles on approach and departure
2. Land in a smaller-than-normal area
3. Possibly hover OGE to determine landing feasibility

### Recon Orbit Procedure

Before landing in any confined area:

| Step | Action | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **High recon** (500 ft AGL or higher) — orbit the area | Identify the LZ; identify obstacles; determine wind direction |
| 2 | **Low recon** (100–150 ft AGL) — orbit the area | Confirm surface conditions; wire hazard check; identify escape route |
| 3 | Select **approach direction** — into the wind | Maintain a safe abort path throughout |
| 4 | Identify **escape route** if approach must be aborted | Confirm clear of obstacles |
| 5 | Brief the approach verbally (or in the student's head) | "I'll approach from the south at 20°; abort right if ..." |
| 6 | **Execute approach** — or wave off if any doubt | Never commit to a confined area approach without a viable abort |

> *A confined area that isn't scouted is an ambush. Always do the recon. Always have the abort route identified before you begin the approach.*

## Common Errors

| Error | Correction |
|---|---|
| **Abeam point missed — overshooting final** | "Start your descent the moment the pad is at 9 or 3 o'clock. If you fly past it, your final will be too long." |
| **Too fast on short final — can't terminate** | "If you're at 30 KIAS at 50 feet, you're going too fast. Either go-around or accept a running landing." |
| **Not adding collective exiting ETL on final** | "When you slow through 20 knots on final, you fall through ETL. The lift goes away. Add collective before it takes you down." |
| **Salvaging a bad approach instead of going around** | "Go-around is never wrong. A bad approach that you try to fix almost always gets worse. Commit early." |
| **Lowslope skid first on slope landing** | "Upslope first. Always. Downslope first gives you nothing to stop the rollover." |
| **Lateral cyclic toward downslope during slope landing** | "Cyclic INTO the slope while landing. Cyclic away from the slope with a skid on the ground WILL roll you over." |
| **No recon before confined area approach** | "You never fly into a confined area without knowing your escape route. Orbit first — always." |
