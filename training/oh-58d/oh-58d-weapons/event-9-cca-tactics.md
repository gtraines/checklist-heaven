## Learning Objectives

Upon completion of this event the student will be able to:
- [ ] Distinguish Close Combat Attack (CCA) from Close Air Support (CAS) and explain why the OH-58D conducts CCA under Army doctrine
- [ ] Select a Battle Position (BP) using the criteria of fields of fire, cover, masking, and covered routes; identify primary, alternate, and supplementary positions
- [ ] Execute the hull-down engagement cycle within the 60-second time standard
- [ ] Develop an engagement area using target reference points, trigger lines, and sectors of fire
- [ ] Execute Scout/Attack team tactics (OH-58D as designator for Apache) and Hunter/Killer team tactics (two Kiowas)
- [ ] Execute the pop-up attack profile with apex time < 15 seconds
- [ ] Conduct target handoff using grid, TRP-based, laser-based, and talk-on methods
- [ ] Apply engagement priorities (AD → armor → C2 → soft targets)

## Part 1 — CCA vs. CAS: Doctrinal Distinction

| Concept | CAS — Close Air Support | CCA — Close Combat Attack |
|---------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Definition** | Air action against hostile targets in close proximity to friendly forces, requiring detailed integration with the fire and movement of those forces (Joint doctrine) | Direct fire support to ground maneuver forces by Army aviation, integrated through Army tactical command and control (Army doctrine) |
| **Who Conducts** | Fixed-wing aircraft (A-10, F/A-18, F-16); joint tasking | Army rotary-wing (AH-64, OH-58D) as organic maneuver elements |
| **Control** | Requires a JTAC; formal 9-line brief | Ground maneuver commander via aviation unit command net; less formal procedures |
| **Integration** | Joint operation; fires coordinated through ASOC/DASC | Organic Army fires; coordinated directly between aviation and ground maneuver units |
| **OH-58D Role** | Not typically assigned formal CAS (lacks JTAC integration) | Conducts CCA as an organic Army Aviation scout/attack asset |

> **Key Takeaway:** When an OH-58D Kiowa attacks enemy forces in support of ground troops, it is conducting **CCA, not CAS.** The coordination procedures are Army-centric (ground commander → aviation team leader → flight lead → wingman), not joint (JTAC → pilot). This distinction changes control procedures, communication flows, and coordination authority.

> *⚠ Realism Divergence: In DCS multiplayer, the OH-58D may operate in a mixed environment where JTACs (human or AI) use 9-line CAS procedures. While doctrinally incorrect for CCA, students should be familiar with both formats since DCS missions may use either. The tactical principles — fire and maneuver, deconfliction, positive identification — remain identical regardless of the doctrinal label.*

## Part 2 — Battle Position Operations

A **Battle Position (BP)** is a position from which the OH-58D observes and engages targets in an assigned engagement area.

### Selecting a Battle Position

| Criterion | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Fields of Fire** | Clear LOS to the engagement area; MMS must be able to observe and designate at engagement ranges (1–8 km) |
| **Cover & Concealment** | Terrain cover for hull-down; fuselage maskable behind terrain or vegetation |
| **Mask** | A ridgeline, treeline, or building that permits hull-down exposure of MMS only while fuselage is protected |
| **Routes In/Out** | Covered approach and departure routes that avoid skylining or exposing the aircraft |
| **Alternate & Supplementary BPs** | Pre-plan at least 2 alternates; never plan to fight from a single position |
| **Distance from EA** | Optimum 2–5 km from targets; within Hellfire range (1–8 km) |

**Never skyline the aircraft.** Approach and depart BPs so that terrain (not sky) is the background. An aircraft silhouetted against the sky is visible for miles.

### Hull-Down Engagement Cycle

| Step | Action | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| 1 | **Approach BP** | NOE flight; terrain masked; arrive from covered route |
| 2 | **Hover into hull-down** | Rise until MMS clears terrain mask; fuselage remains below cover |
| 3 | **Scan EA** | MMS in WFOV; systematic search of assigned sector |
| 4 | **Detect & Identify Target** | MMS to NFOV; PID; classify as hostile per ROE |
| 5 | **Engage** | LOBL Hellfire (primary); APKWS or rockets for lighter targets |
| 6 | **BDA** | Observe impact; confirm destroyed or assess damage |
| 7 | **Remask** | Descend below terrain; do not linger in hull-down after BDA |
| 8 | **Displace** | After 1–2 engagements from same position, move to alternate BP |

### Hull-Down Time Standards

| Standard | Duration |
|----------|----------|
| **Maximum (hard limit)** | 60 seconds per unmask cycle |
| **Optimal** | 20–30 seconds (pre-planned: know where to look, shoot quickly, remask) |
| **Rapid Shot** | 10–15 seconds (pre-sighted target, LOBL lock, fire, guide, remask) |

> **Instructor Note:** Students consistently spend too long in hull-down. They search slowly, adjust weapon settings while exposed, and delay remasking. Drill the cycle: **unmask → scan → engage → BDA → remask.** Treat hull-down time like a breath-hold — the clock starts the moment you clear the mask, and 60 seconds is the hard limit regardless of whether all targets are engaged.

## Part 3 — Engagement Area Operations

An **Engagement Area (EA)** is a pre-planned geographic area where the commander intends to destroy an enemy force using massed fires.

### EA Development Elements

| Element | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **EA Boundary** | Defined geographic limits where targets are expected and fires are authorized |
| **Target Reference Points (TRPs)** | Pre-designated points within the EA for rapid target handoff ("From TRP 1, south 300 m, 2 vehicles") |
| **Trigger Lines** | Lines that initiate specific engagements; when enemy crosses a trigger line, designated weapons fire |
| **Engagement Priorities** | Pre-assigned priority: e.g., "Priority 1: air defense; Priority 2: armor; Priority 3: soft targets" |
| **Weapons Allocation** | Which weapons are assigned to which target types within the EA |

### Sector of Fire Assignment (Multi-Ship)

| Aircraft | Sector | Battle Position |
|----------|--------|----------------|
| **Lead (Kiowa 1)** | Left half of EA | BP Alpha — left flank |
| **Wing (Kiowa 2)** | Right half of EA | BP Bravo — right flank |
| **Overlap zone** | ~10° in center | Coordination required for targets in overlap |

### Engagement Priority Table

| Priority | Target Type | Weapon | Rationale |
|----------|------------|--------|-----------|
| 1 | **Air Defense** (SA-8, ZSU-23-4, MANPADS teams) | Hellfire K or M | Destroying enemy AD protects aircraft and ground forces |
| 2 | **Armor** (MBTs, IFVs) | Hellfire K | Armor is the primary threat to friendly ground maneuver |
| 3 | **C2 Vehicles** (command posts, comms) | Hellfire M or APKWS | Disrupting command degrades the entire enemy formation |
| 4 | **Soft Targets** (trucks, troops, logistics) | Rockets, .50 cal, APKWS | Lower priority; lower-cost weapons appropriate |

## Part 4 — Team Tactics

### Scout/Attack Team (OH-58D + AH-64 Apache)

| Role | Aircraft | Function |
|------|----------|----------|
| **Scout** | OH-58D Kiowa | Finds, identifies, and designates targets; provides laser designation for Apache Hellfire shots |
| **Attack** | AH-64 Apache | Fires Hellfire guided by the Kiowa's laser; provides heavy fires from covered position |

**Tactical Advantage:** The Kiowa takes the risk of forward observation; the Apache remains masked farther back with far greater firepower. The Kiowa's small size and mast-mounted sight make it significantly harder to detect than the Apache.

### Scout/Attack Procedure

| Step | Kiowa (Scout) | Apache (Attack) |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| 1 | Advance to BP; hull-down observation of EA | Remain masked; monitor scout radio |
| 2 | Detect and identify target | — |
| 3 | "Tally [target], [TRP/grid], preparing to lase, code [PRF]" | "Copy, code [PRF], standing by" |
| 4 | "Lasing" — begin laser designation | — |
| 5 | Maintain laser; MMS on target | "Rifle" — Hellfire fired |
| 6 | Maintain laser until: "Splash" | — |
| 7 | BDA; report results | — |
| 8 | Next target; repeat cycle | Rearm/remask between shots |

### Hunter/Killer Team (OH-58D + OH-58D)

| Role | Aircraft | Function |
|------|----------|----------|
| **Hunter** | Kiowa 1 | Finds and designates targets; provides laser; may also self-engage with Hellfire |
| **Killer** | Kiowa 2 | Engages designated targets; receives buddy-lase or self-engages separate targets |

**Employment:**
- Both aircraft occupy separate BPs with overlapping sectors of fire
- The Hunter finds targets and designates for the Killer
- After the Killer shoots, roles can swap — continuous observation and engagement with one aircraft always covering
- This approach maintains constant pressure on the enemy while providing mutual protection

### Multi-Ship Coordination Standards

| Element | Standard |
|---------|----------|
| **BP Spacing** | 500–2,000 m separation; maintain visual contact or radio coordination |
| **Communication** | Internal team frequency; rapid target handoff using TRPs and clock calls |
| **Deconfliction** | Lateral (separate BPs and sectors) — never cross each other's engagement arcs |
| **Weapons Status** | **Hold:** Fire only in self-defense; **Tight:** Fire only at positively identified hostile targets; **Free:** Fire at any target not confirmed friendly |

### Clock Call Format (Target Handoff Between Team Members)

> "Contact, [target type], [clock position from lead], [range], [description/activity]."
>
> *Example: "Contact, BTR, two o'clock, three klicks, moving south on road."*

## Part 5 — Rotary-Wing Attack Profiles

| Profile | Description | Best For | Exposure Level |
|---------|-------------|----------|---------------|
| **Hover Fire — Hull-Down** | Stationary from masked BP; MMS only exposed above cover | Hellfire LOBL; precision observation; maximum stealth | Lowest (MMS only) |
| **Running Fire** | Engagement during lateral/forward flight at 30–80 KIAS | Rockets, .50 cal, Hellfire during displacement | Moderate (aircraft exposed during run) |
| **Diving Fire** | 10–20° dive toward target | Hydra 70 rockets; .50 cal strafing | High (unmasked, descending toward threat) |
| **Pop-Up Attack** | NOE approach → rapid climb to weapons release → engage → descend | Any weapon; surprise; minimal exposure at apex | Moderate-High (brief exposure at apex) |

### Pop-Up Attack Procedure

| Step | Action | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| 1 | **NOE approach** toward target area | Terrain masked; navigate to planned IP by map/GPS |
| 2 | **At Initial Point (IP): climb rapidly** | Full collective; 40–60 KIAS during climb |
| 3 | **At apex (300–800 ft AGL): acquire target** | Quick MMS scan; target at pre-briefed bearing and range |
| 4 | **Engage** | Rockets, gun, or snap LOBL Hellfire shot |
| 5 | **Immediately push over and descend** | Break turn + descent; NOE egress |
| 6 | **Egress along pre-planned covered route** | Do NOT return along the same ingress route |

**Apex Time Standard:** Target < 15 seconds from climb initiation to nose-over descent. Anything longer exposes the aircraft as a stationary target.

> **Instructor Note:** The pop-up attack must be pre-planned. Students who "wing it" spend 30+ seconds at the apex trying to find the target — this is fatal in a threat environment. Brief the IP location, climb heading, expected target bearing and distance from apex, and the egress route before takeoff. Fly the plan.

## Part 6 — Target Handoff Procedures

### Grid-Based Handoff

> "Target at grid [6- or 8-digit grid], [target description], [activity]."
>
> *Example: "Target at grid NV 3847 5621, two T-72 tanks, stationary in treeline."*

**Requires:** MMS coordinate extraction via LRF + GPS; transmitted to receiving element via radio.

### TRP-Based Handoff

> "From TRP [number], [direction], [distance], [description]."
>
> *Example: "From TRP 3, east 500 meters, BMP in defilade."*

**Advantage:** Fastest method when TRPs are pre-briefed; no grid required; both parties have TRPs marked on their maps.

### Laser-Based Handoff

> "Lasing, code [PRF], [target description/bearing from reference]."

**Used for:** Direct handoff to a Hellfire shooter (Apache, wingman) for buddy-lased Hellfire or APKWS.

### Talk-On Procedure (Ground to Air)

When ground forces direct the aircraft onto a target they can see but the aircraft cannot:

| Step | Ground Element | OH-58D |
|------|---------------|--------|
| 1 | "From my position, look [direction]" | Slew MMS to indicated bearing from ground position |
| 2 | "Range [distance], [terrain feature]" | Narrow MMS search to indicated range and feature |
| 3 | "Target is [description] at [specific landmark]" | Scan MMS for described target |
| 4 | — | "Tally target" — confirms visual acquisition on MMS |
| 5 | "Cleared hot" or "Cleared to engage" | Engage with appropriate weapon |

## Completion Standards

The student passes this event when they can, without instructor prompting:

- [ ] State the doctrinal distinction between CCA and CAS and identify the control authority difference
- [ ] Select a valid Battle Position during map study, citing at minimum three BP selection criteria (fields of fire, cover, routes in/out)
- [ ] Execute the hull-down engagement cycle within the **60-second time standard** — **exceeding 60 seconds in hull-down on any pass is an automatic REPEAT**
- [ ] Displace to an alternate BP after each 1–2 engagements without instructor prompting
- [ ] Execute a pop-up attack with apex time < 15 seconds — brief the IP, expected target bearing, and egress before execution
- [ ] Demonstrate target handoff using TRP-based format with correct brevity to the instructor (simulating wingman)
- [ ] Apply correct engagement priorities (AD first, then armor, then C2, then soft) when presented with a mixed target set by the instructor
- [ ] Execute Scout/Attack or Hunter/Killer team procedures if multi-ship flight is conducted — correct communication flow and laser handoff procedures

## Common Errors

| Error | Consequence | Correction |
|-------|-------------|------------|
| Not planning alternate BPs | Stuck in one position; enemy identifies and suppresses | Plan Primary, Alternate, Supplementary; displace after 1–2 engagements |
| Engaging without PID | Fratricide; engaging friendly or civilian vehicles | Always PID per ROE before firing; use MMS NFOV and TV sensor for identification |
| Not coordinating sectors in multi-ship | Both aircraft engage same target; other threats escape | Brief sectors before mission; TRP-based assignment; maintain communications |
| Skylining the aircraft | Aircraft visible from below; easy target for all threat types | Approach BPs from behind terrain; ensure terrain (not sky) is the aircraft background |
| Engaging beyond effective range | Low Pk; wasted ordnance | Hellfire: 2–6 km optimal; rockets: 1.5–3 km; gun: < 1.5 km — do not fire beyond these |
| Not conducting BDA; re-engaging destroyed targets | Wasting ordnance on confirmed kills | BDA each strike; confirm destroyed before moving to next target |
| Predictable engagement pattern | Enemy anticipates approach; lays ambush at expected BP | Vary routes, BPs, and timing; never be predictable |
| Target fixation through MMS | Aircraft drifts; altitude/airspeed decay; exits hull-down unintentionally | Cross-check instruments during MMS; maintain positional awareness; designate as habit |
| Un-briefed pop-up attack | Long exposure at apex searching for target; fatal in threat environment | Brief target location, IP, bearing from apex, and egress before flight — never improvise |
